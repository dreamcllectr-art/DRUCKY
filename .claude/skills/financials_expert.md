## The Financials Investment Framework

### Net Interest Margin (NIM) — The Core Driver

NIM is to banks what same-store sales is to retailers — the single most important metric:

**NIM Mechanics:**
- NIM = (Interest Income - Interest Expense) / Average Earning Assets
- Asset-sensitive banks benefit from rate RISES (assets reprice faster than liabilities)
- Liability-sensitive banks benefit from rate CUTS (funding costs drop faster)

**Current NIM Cycle Position:**
- Post-2022 rate hikes: NIMs expanded sharply as loan rates rose
- Deposit betas lagging: Banks keeping excess spread (but betas accelerating)
- Yield curve shape matters: Steeper curve = wider NIM, inverted curve = compressed NIM

**Key signal**: When the yield curve steepens (2s10s spread widens), bank stocks rally 15-25% over the following 6 months. The 2s10s spread is the single best predictor of bank stock returns.

### The Credit Cycle (Where Alpha Lives)

Credit quality moves in predictable cycles that the market systematically misprices:

**Phase 1: Late-Cycle Deterioration**
- Early-stage delinquencies rising (30-60 day past due)
- Management increasing provision for credit losses
- Market PANICS — bank stocks sell off 20-30%
- **Reality**: Reserves being built = future earnings buffer

**Phase 2: Peak Provisions**
- Charge-offs accelerating
- Provision expense at maximum → earnings at minimum
- Market extrapolates losses forever
- **This is when you BUY.** Peak provision ≈ trough earnings ≈ peak fear

**Phase 3: Reserve Release**
- Losses come in below reserving → excess reserves released into earnings
- Reserve release = "free" earnings that boost EPS for 4-6 quarters
- Market initially skeptical ("low quality earnings") then re-rates
- **Strongest returns period.** Earnings accelerating + multiple expanding

**Phase 4: Late Recovery**
- Credit pristine, lending standards loosening
- NIMs compressing as competition increases
- Banks return to aggressive capital return (buybacks, dividends)
- **Approaching top.** Complacency = next cycle seeds being planted

### Capital Return (The Buyback Machine)

Banks are the largest buyers of their own stock in the market:

**Capital return framework:**
- CET1 ratio: Common Equity Tier 1 capital / Risk-Weighted Assets
- Regulatory minimum: ~4.5% + stress capital buffer (varies by bank, typically 2.5-4.5%)
- Excess capital = CET1 above target → returned via buybacks + dividends
- JPM, BAC, WFC collectively buying back $40-60B/year in stock

**Key signal**: When a bank's CET1 ratio exceeds its internal target by >100bps AND credit losses are trending down, the buyback accelerates. 3-5% of market cap in annual buybacks = structural EPS tailwind.

### The Insurance Hard Market

Insurance is deeply cyclical with multi-year pricing cycles:

**Hard market characteristics (current phase):**
- Premium rates rising 5-15% annually
- Insurers being selective (tightening terms and conditions)
- Combined ratios improving (< 95% = profitable underwriting)
- Hard markets typically last 3-5 years after major loss events

**Why this matters:**
- PGR, TRV, CB are printing record underwriting profits
- Market consensus typically assumes reversion to soft market too quickly
- Catastrophe reinsurance costs rising → passed through to consumers → structural inflation protection

**Key signal**: When the combined ratio is trending below 93% AND premium growth exceeds loss cost trends, the hard market has legs.

## Earnings Call Intelligence

**What to listen for (banks):**
1. "Net interest income" trajectory — absolute dollars, not just NIM %
2. "Provision" vs "net charge-offs" — provision > NCOs = building reserves (future cushion)
3. "Deposit costs" — beta acceleration? Non-interest bearing mix shift?
4. "CET1" target and actual — excess capital = buyback capacity
5. "Loan growth" — demand-driven (good) vs supply-driven/loosening standards (risky)

**What to listen for (insurance):**
1. "Combined ratio" — below 95% = making money on underwriting
2. "Rate adequacy" — are rate increases exceeding loss cost trends?
3. "Cat losses" — one-time vs structural (climate-driven frequency)
4. "Investment income" — rising rates help the float portfolio

## Risk Factors
- Regulatory: Basel III Endgame rules (higher capital requirements → less buyback capacity)
- Credit events: CRE (commercial real estate) stress in office sector → regional bank exposure
- Liquidity: Deposit flight risk during stress events (SVB showed how fast it can happen)
- Geopolitical: Sanctions, trade wars affect capital markets revenue
